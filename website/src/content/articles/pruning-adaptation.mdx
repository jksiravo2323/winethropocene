## The Cheapest Tool

There are three ways to adapt a vineyard to a climate it was not planted for. You can change the genetics — new disease-resistant varieties, as Bordeaux has begun to. You can change the geography — move north and uphill, as the wine belt is already doing. Both are expensive, slow, and irreversible.

The third way costs nothing extra and uses no water. You change how you cut the vine.

## The Friulian Argument

Marco Simonit grew up in Cormons, in Friuli, and has spent three decades making a single unfashionable case: that the way most of the world prunes its vines is quietly killing them. Conventional pruning leaves large flush cuts on the main trunk. Each cut interrupts the flow of sap and dries out a cone of wood behind it. Over years, those desiccation cones accumulate along the vine's vascular system and choke it.

The method Simonit codified with his partner Pierpaolo Sirch, from the 1990s onward, does the opposite. It rests on a few principles: let the vine branch and spread with age; keep the sap channel continuous; make only small cuts, and only on young wood; and leave a margin of "respect wood" so the drying stays off the main pathway. The wounds are pushed out onto minor offshoots rather than carved into the trunk.

## Why It Matters Now

The point is not nostalgia for old vines. It is that the wounds left by conventional pruning are the main entry route for trunk diseases — above all esca, a fungal complex that hollows the vine from the inside. Esca pressure rises in warmer, wetter seasons, the same conditions pushing Bordeaux toward hybrids. A vine pruned to keep its vascular system intact presents fewer open doors. Decade-long European studies have since confirmed what Simonit argued from the field: pruning method measurably affects a vine's susceptibility to esca.

So this is an adaptation lever with an unusually low price. It needs no irrigation water, no new plant material, and no replanting. It keeps the vines you already have alive and functioning longer under mounting disease pressure. It has spread from Friuli to more than 160 estates worldwide — including Domaine de la Romanée-Conti and Château d'Yquem — across France, California, Australia, Chile, Argentina, and South Africa.

## The Part That Doesn't Get Quoted

In August 2024, Simonit gave a lecture at Harvard. The reporting focused on the pruning. The line worth keeping was the other one.

He said, plainly, that future wine will be made at higher altitudes and latitudes than today. The man whose entire career is a method for keeping existing vineyards healthy does not claim the method saves them. It keeps the current vineyard standing while the conditions for the next one assemble somewhere cooler.

Pruning is adaptation. It is also a way of buying time. Both things are true, and Simonit is the one who says so.
