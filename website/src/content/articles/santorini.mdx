## The Model That Worked for Two Thousand Years

Santorini grows its wine on rainfall. There are no rivers on the island, no aquifer worth the name, and the tap water is not potable. For most of two thousand years that was not a problem, because the vines were built for it.

The vines are trained into *kouloura* — low circular baskets, coiled close to the volcanic ground. The shape shields the fruit from the Meltemi and Sirocco winds and, more importantly, traps the moisture that condenses out of the night air off the Aegean. Some of these baskets are nearly two centuries old. They were engineered, over generations, to drink fog.

The island never irrigated. It never needed to.

Since the 1960s, Santorini's vineyard has contracted from roughly 4,000 hectares to about 1,000. Part of that is drought. Part of it is people: the island now receives approximately 3.4 million visitors a year, and since around 2010 agricultural land has been converting to tourism development. Drought takes the yield; tourism takes the land.

The rainfall figures explain the rest. Annual precipitation has fallen below 200mm for three consecutive years. On an island that depends almost entirely on what falls from the sky, three dry years in a row is not a fluctuation. It is the operating condition.

In 2025 the island picked roughly 450 tonnes of grapes — about 15% of what it picked in 2000. Assyrtiko, the grape Santorini is known for, fell from around 2,500 tonnes in 2022 to about 500 in 2025. In some old-vine parcels yields dropped 60–80%; a few came in under 500 kilos per hectare. At least one estate reported a near-total loss, and some Vinsanto production was paused for want of fruit.

The wine that did get made was, by critical consensus, excellent. This is the strange shape of the crisis: quality has held while quantity has collapsed. With fruit scarce, grape prices climbed to around €10 per kilo. Fewer than two dozen producers are still working the island.

## Why This One Is Different

Most wine regions answer drought the same way: they irrigate. They drill, they pipe in water, they install drip lines. Santorini cannot. There is no water under the island to pump, and the tradition — and the appellation logic — is built on dry-farming. As one Domaine Sigalas winemaker put it, the work now is simply trying to replace the water that no longer arrives in winter.

That is what makes Santorini the clearest case on the map. It is viticulture with the irrigation option removed — a region that has to adapt with technique, plant material, and land-use decisions, or not at all. Growers at Argyros Estate have said openly that survival now requires "ground-breaking decisions," and the word that keeps surfacing in conversations among producers is *extinction*.

It may not come to that. But Santorini is where the usual escape hatch is bolted shut. The island that taught its vines to drink fog is running short of fog — and there is nothing underneath to fall back on.
