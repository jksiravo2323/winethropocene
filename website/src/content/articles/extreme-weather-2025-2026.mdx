## Overview

The 2025–2026 growing season in the northern hemisphere compressed four of wine's most destructive climate threats into a single year. In April 2025, a late frost swept through Burgundy and Champagne, burning young buds after an unusually warm March had brought them out early. In May, hailstorms tore through Bordeaux's Right Bank, shredding leaves and young clusters across several hundred hectares. By July, Mendoza was in its fourth consecutive year of drought — irrigation reservoirs at critically low levels, the Andes snowpack having declined roughly 30% since 1990. And in August, wildfire smoke from blazes in northern California drifted over Napa and Sonoma, raising the spectre of smoke-tainted fruit just weeks before harvest.

Four threats. Two continents. One growing season. This is not an outlier. It is the convergence of trends that climate scientists have been tracking for two decades.

## The Four Disasters

### 1. Frost — Burgundy and Champagne, April 2025

After an unseasonably warm March pushed budbreak forward by nearly three weeks in parts of Burgundy, temperatures dropped below -4°C on the nights of 6–7 April. According to the BIVB, damage was concentrated in the Côte de Beaune and Chablis, where some producers reported losses of 50–80% of their potential crop. Champagne was hit less severely but still saw significant damage in the Côte des Bar.

Frost is not new to these regions. What has changed is the sequence: warmer winters → earlier budbreak → greater exposure to spring frost. The same warming that is expanding the wine map northward is making traditional regions more vulnerable to frost damage — a paradox of climate change in viticulture.

### 2. Hail — Bordeaux, May 2025

On 20 May, a series of severe thunderstorms crossed the Gironde estuary, dropping hailstones large enough to strip leaves and puncture young grape clusters. The worst damage was concentrated in Pomerol, Fronsac, and parts of Saint-Émilion. The Bordeaux wine council (CIVB) estimated that approximately 4,000 hectares were affected, with some parcels losing their entire crop.

Hail has been a perennial risk in Bordeaux, but the frequency of severe hailstorms has increased. Warmer, more humid air masses in spring create more intense convection — the same physics that drives more extreme rainfall events across Europe.

### 3. Drought — Mendoza, Argentina, July 2025

Mendoza entered the 2025–2026 season in its fourth consecutive year of drought. The Andes snowpack — the region's primary water source — has declined by approximately 30% since 1990, according to the Argentine Institute for Snow Research. Irrigation reservoirs serving the Uco Valley and Luján de Cuyo were at 35–45% of capacity by mid-winter.

The drought affects not just yields but wine style. With less water, berries are smaller, skins thicker, and sugars concentrate faster — producing higher-alcohol wines with more tannin. Some producers are adapting with dry-farming techniques and drought-resistant rootstocks. Others are abandoning marginal parcels.

### 4. Wildfire Smoke — California, August 2025

In August, wildfires in northern California — driven by a hot, dry summer and abundant fuel from previous wet winters — sent smoke drifting over Napa and Sonoma valleys. Smoke exposure during veraison and ripening can lead to smoke-tainted wines, with compounds like guaiacol and 4-methylguaiacol binding to grape sugars and being released during fermentation.

By late August, several producers had submitted fruit samples for smoke-taint analysis. Early results were mixed — some blocks tested below sensory thresholds, others showed elevated levels. The full impact on the 2025 California vintage will take years to assess, as some smoke compounds only become apparent after bottle ageing.

## What This Means

None of these threats is new in isolation. Frost, hail, drought, and wildfire smoke have always been risks in viticulture. What has changed is their simultaneity and their frequency. Four threats across two continents in a single growing season is not a freak event — it is the logical outcome of a warming atmosphere.

The 2025–2026 season is a preview of what viticulture will contend with routinely by mid-century. Adaptation — frost candles, hail nets, drought-resistant rootstocks, smoke-taint testing — can mitigate individual threats. But the convergence of multiple threats in a single season tests the limits of what adaptation can achieve.

The question is not whether wine will survive. It will. The question is where, what varieties, and who can afford to keep growing them.
