## The Decision

In 2025–2026, France's INAO — the authority that governs the Bordeaux AOC system — approved six disease-resistant hybrid grape varieties for use in Bordeaux wines: Sauvignac, Floréal, Vidoc Noir, Voltis, Souvignier Gris, and (in a second tranche) additional varieties still under review.

These are PIWI hybrids (*Pilzwiderstandsfähig* — German for "fungus-resistant"), bred by crossing traditional *Vitis vinifera* with other *Vitis* species to introduce natural disease resistance. They are not traditional Bordeaux grapes. They were created in laboratories. And they are now, officially, permitted in Bordeaux AOC wines.

## Why Now

Bordeaux's traditional varieties — Merlot, Cabernet Sauvignon, Cabernet Franc — evolved in a cooler, drier climate than the one they now inhabit. Average temperatures in the region have risen 0.9–1.2°C since pre-industrial levels. Harvest dates are two to three weeks earlier than in 1980. Warmer, wetter growing seasons are increasing pressure from the three major fungal diseases: downy mildew (*Plasmopara viticola*), powdery mildew (*Erysiphe necator*), and botrytis bunch rot (*Botrytis cinerea*).

Each year, Bordeaux producers apply more fungicide to keep these diseases at bay. The hybrids offer an alternative: they can reduce fungicide applications by up to 60–80% compared to traditional varieties in high-pressure seasons. They were selected specifically for resistance to all three of Bordeaux's major fungal threats.

## The Fine Print

The approvals come with deliberately restrictive caps:

- **5%** of any producer's total vineyard area
- **10%** of any finished blend

The hybrids are classified as "accessory" grapes. They cannot appear on the label. They form part of Bordeaux's official VIFA list (Varieties of Interest for Climate Adaptation) — a designation that signals their role: an insurance policy, not a replacement.

For a 20-hectare estate, the 5% cap means one hectare of hybrids at most. The 10% blend cap means no wine can be majority-hybrid. These are minority components — present, but anonymous.

## The 2050 Vintage, Already Bottled

The regulator's response is restriction. One château's response is a rehearsal. At Château La Tour Carnet, in the Haut-Médoc, the owner Bernard Magrez has spent more than a decade trying to taste the future before it arrives.

The project — branded "La Tour Carnet 2050" — runs a vineyard plot wired with electric heating cables and equipment borrowed from the aeronautics industry to raise its temperature toward the +2°C to +4°C that Bordeaux is projected to see by mid-century. The traditional varieties — Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Cabernet Franc — are grown under that accelerated climate to see how their ripening, acidity, sugar, and alcohol behave when the weather is moved forward thirty years.

Alongside the heated plot, the estate has planted a collection of more than 80 grape varieties since 2013 — many of them "forgotten" old varieties — and vinifies each separately in a cellar of 84 temperature-controlled vats. The aim is to find the grapes that hold a recognisably Bordeaux profile under a climate Bordeaux does not yet have.

It is the same question the INAO is asking, approached from the opposite end. The regulator permits 5% and waits for evidence. The château manufactures the evidence, one heated row at a time.

## What This Signals

The INAO's decision is significant not because of the volume — 5% is a rounding error — but because of the symbolism. Bordeaux is the world's most regulated, most traditional, most copied wine region. Its AOC system was codified in 1936 and has resisted change for nearly a century. Permitting lab-bred grapes into it, even at 5%, is an acknowledgment that the climate for which the AOC was drawn no longer exists.

Bordeaux is not alone. Champagne approved Voltis — one of the same PIWI varieties — in 2021. Other French AOCs are following similar paths. But Bordeaux carries symbolic weight. If hybrids are permitted here, they can be permitted anywhere.

## The Open Questions

The 5% cap is explicitly framed as experimental. It can be raised, lowered, or revoked based on evidence. The open questions are:

- Will producers actually plant hybrids, or will the approval remain theoretical?
- Will the cap be raised if disease pressure continues to increase?
- What do these varieties taste like — and will consumers accept them in a Bordeaux blend?

The first question is the most urgent. Planting a vineyard is a 30-year decision. A 5% allocation is not a commitment — it is a trial. The real story will unfold over the next decade.

Bordeaux has not embraced hybrids. It has opened a door. The question is whether 5% is enough to walk through it.
