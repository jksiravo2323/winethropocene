## The Decision

In 2025–2026, France's INAO — the authority that governs the Bordeaux AOC system — approved six disease-resistant hybrid grape varieties for use in Bordeaux wines: Sauvignac, Floréal, Vidoc Noir, Voltis, Souvignier Gris, and (in a second tranche) additional varieties still under review.

The approval is modest in scope:
- Capped at **5% of vineyard area** per estate
- No more than **10% of any final blend**
- The varieties do not appear on front labels

But the symbolism is enormous. Bordeaux — the most regulated, most imitated wine region on Earth — has acknowledged that its traditional varieties cannot cope with the disease pressure of a warmer, wetter climate without chemical intervention. The region that gave the world Cabernet Sauvignon and Merlot is now turning to lab-bred hybrids developed in Germany and Switzerland.

## Why This Matters

### Disease Pressure Is Rising

Warmer winters mean fungal pathogens overwinter more successfully. Wetter springs create ideal conditions for downy mildew (*Plasmopara viticola*) and powdery mildew (*Erysiphe necator*). In Bordeaux's maritime-influenced climate, the number of high-pressure disease days per season has increased by roughly 15–25% since the 1980s.

Conventional viticulture has responded with more fungicide sprays — up to 15–20 treatments per season in some years. Organic and biodynamic producers, who rely on copper and sulphur, face even greater challenges. Copper accumulates in soils; sulphur washes off in rain.

### The PIWI Solution

PIWI (from the German *pilzwiderstandsfähig*, "fungus-resistant") varieties are hybrids bred by crossing European *Vitis vinifera* with American and Asian *Vitis* species that carry natural disease resistance. The result: grapes that need 70–90% fewer fungicide treatments.

The six approved varieties are:

| Variety | Type | Origin |
|---|---|---|
| Sauvignac | White | Freiburg, Germany |
| Floréal | White | INRAE, France |
| Souvignier Gris | White / Gris | Freiburg, Germany |
| Voltis | White | INRAE, France |
| Vidoc Noir | Red | Freiburg, Germany |

Early tastings suggest these varieties can produce wines of genuine quality — crisp whites with good acidity, and structured reds with moderate alcohol — suitable for Bordeaux's climate and cuisine.

## The Politics

The INAO's decision was not made lightly. It followed years of lobbying by the Syndicat des Vignerons de Bordeaux, the Conseil Interprofessionnel du Vin de Bordeaux (CIVB), and INRAE researchers. Opposition came from traditionalists who argued that any hybrid, however carefully bred, dilutes Bordeaux's identity.

The compromise — caps on area and blend percentage — reflects this tension. The hybrids are an insurance policy, not a replacement. For now, they are invisible to consumers.

But the caps are likely a starting point. If disease pressure continues to rise and fungicide regulations tighten (as they are doing across the EU), the economic logic of PIWI varieties will become harder to resist. Several other French AOCs — including those in the Loire and Languedoc — are watching Bordeaux closely.

## The Bigger Picture

Bordeaux's decision is part of a broader reckoning in European viticulture. The classic varieties that built the world's great wine regions — Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Chardonnay, Pinot Noir — were selected in a cooler, more stable climate. That climate is disappearing.

The options are: (1) more chemical inputs, (2) move north, (3) change varieties. Bordeaux — the most conservative of regions — just chose option 3, however cautiously.

This is not just about Bordeaux. It is about whether Europe's entire AOC/PDO system — built on the premise of fixed geography and fixed varieties — can adapt to a moving climate.
