## The Numbers

Global wine-growing suitability is shifting poleward by **0.5° to 1.0° of latitude per decade** — roughly 55 to 110 kilometres every ten years. The direction is consistent across models: the equatorward edge of the wine belt is contracting as temperatures exceed viability thresholds for traditional varieties, while the poleward edge is expanding into regions previously too cold for viticulture.

The net effect: the classic 30°–50° wine belt (spanning both hemispheres) is compressing on the warm side and stretching on the cool side. Some regions will exit wine production; others will enter it. The map of wine is being redrawn in real time.

## The Winners

### England and Denmark

English wine production has grown exponentially: acreage has quadrupled in 20 years, and English sparkling wine now routinely beats Champagne in blind tastings. The chalk soils of southern England are geologically identical to those of Champagne — and the climate is moving toward Champagne's historical norms.

Denmark, with 3,500 hectares of commercial vineyard, is one of the newest entrants to the wine world. Solaris and other cold-hardy PIWI varieties dominate, but as temperatures rise, classic varieties like Pinot Noir are becoming viable. Denmark produced its first commercial still red wine from Pinot Noir in 2023.

### Canada and the Pacific Northwest

Canada's wine regions — the Okanagan Valley, Niagara Peninsula, and Prince Edward County — are among the fastest-growing in the world. Rising temperatures have shifted the frost-free growing season, and Canadian Cabernet Franc and Riesling are winning international awards.

### Patagonia and Tasmania

In the southern hemisphere, the shift is toward the pole: Patagonia (Argentina) and Tasmania (Australia) are gaining suitability as traditional regions like Mendoza and the Barossa Valley face increasing heat and water stress.

## The Losers

### Southern Europe

The Mediterranean basin — Spain, southern Italy, Greece, Cyprus — is experiencing declining suitability for traditional varieties. Rising temperatures, declining rainfall, and more frequent heatwaves are pushing these regions toward the upper thermal limits for Vitis vinifera. Some areas are adapting with drought-resistant varieties and canopy management. Others are abandoning marginal vineyards.

### California's Central Valley

The Central Valley, which produces the majority of California's wine by volume, faces a dual threat: rising temperatures and declining water availability. The region's bulk-wine model — high-volume, irrigated production — is increasingly unsustainable under projected climate scenarios.

## What This Means

The wine belt is not just moving — it is stratifying. Premium wine regions at higher latitudes and elevations are gaining. Bulk-wine regions at lower latitudes and elevations are losing. The economics of this shift will reshape the global wine industry over the next 30 years.

The question is not whether wine will survive climate change. It will. The question is where, what varieties, and who can afford to grow them.
