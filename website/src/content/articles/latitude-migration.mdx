## The Numbers

Global climate models converge on a single, startling figure: wine-growing suitability is shifting poleward by 0.5° to 1° of latitude per decade. In the Northern Hemisphere, that is 55 to 110 kilometres per decade — roughly the distance from Bordeaux to the Loire Valley, repeated every ten years.

The INRAE Global Wine Suitability Index, the leading academic model for this question, shows a 10–20% reduction in suitability for classic varieties in the southernmost zones of their historic range. The changes are already measurable. Harvest dates in classic European regions have advanced two to three weeks since 1980. Sugar levels have risen by 2–4 g/L (°Brix). Total acidity has fallen by 10–20%. Alcohol potential is up 0.5–1% ABV.

This is not a prediction. It is a description of the present.

## What's Moving Where

The shift is variety-specific. Cool-climate grapes — Pinot Noir, Chardonnay, Riesling, Sauvignon Blanc — are losing suitability at their historic southern range edges and gaining it further north. Heat-tolerant varieties — Touriga Nacional, Bobal, Solaris — are expanding into the newly warm southern zones.

Pinot Noir's core historic band — 45° to 48°N, the latitude of Burgundy and Oregon — is projected to lose suitability below approximately 38°N and gain it at 50° to 55°N. That band includes southern England, Denmark, Jutland, and southern Sweden. Cabernet Sauvignon, historically at home between 38° and 44°N (Bordeaux, Napa), is losing suitability below roughly 30°N and gaining at 45° to 50°N — central France, northern Italy, and southern Canada, including the Okanagan Valley and Michigan.

## The Vertical Dimension

Latitude is not the only axis of migration. Altitude functions as a parallel dimension: a 500-metre elevation gain roughly equals a 0.5° northward latitude shift in temperature terms. Argentine wineries are already exploiting this equation, establishing vineyards at 1,800 metres in the Andes to replace low-altitude sites that are becoming too warm.

The relationship is linear: go up, or go north. Both move you into cooler conditions. Both have limits — mountains have peaks, and the planet has a finite northern landmass.

## What's Arriving

The emerging cool-climate regions are not hypothetical. UK vineyard acreage has grown significantly since 2000, concentrated in Sussex and Kent. Denmark now has commercial-scale plantings in Jutland. Sweden has vineyards in Skåne. Norway has experimental plantings near the Oslo fjord. Estonia and the Baltic coast of Germany are seeing new vineyard development.

These are not curiosity plantings. They are commercial vineyards producing wines that are winning international awards. English sparkling wine now routinely outperforms Champagne in blind tastings — a result that would have been unthinkable thirty years ago, when the idea of English wine was a punchline.

## What's Being Left Behind

The southernmost zones of historic wine regions are under measurable stress. Southern Spain, southern Italy, and parts of France's Languedoc are losing suitability for traditional varieties. The 10–20% reduction shown by the INRAE index is an average; specific sites are more severely affected.

This does not mean these regions will stop producing wine. It means they will produce different wine — from different varieties, using different techniques, at different yields. The adaptation is already underway, from Bordeaux's hybrid grape experiment to Spanish producers reviving heat-tolerant heritage varieties like Bobal.

## The Map Is Being Redrawn

The wine belt is not a fixed feature of the planet. It is a historical accident — the set of latitudes where, in the climate of the 20th century, *Vitis vinifera* could ripen reliably enough to support a commercial industry. That climate is changing. The belt is moving. And every other wine-and-climate story — disease pressure, water scarcity, varietal extinction, the economics of adaptation — unfolds on this moving map.

The question is not whether wine regions will shift. They already are. The question is whether the institutions, regulations, and capital that define the wine industry can move as fast as the climate.
