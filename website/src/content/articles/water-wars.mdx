## The Water Footprint of Wine

One bottle of wine requires approximately **632 litres** of water — from vineyard irrigation to winery cleaning. That figure varies enormously by region, variety, and farming practice, but the order of magnitude is consistent: wine is a water-intensive product, and most of the world's great wine regions depend on irrigation to some degree.

Three of those regions — Chile, Argentina, and California — are now facing simultaneous water crises. Each crisis has its own legal and political character, but all three converge on the same question: who owns the water, and who decides how it is allocated when there is not enough to go around?

## Chile: The Privatised Water Market

Chile is unique among wine-producing nations: its water rights are fully privatised, enshrined in the 1981 Water Code, which separates water ownership from land ownership and allows water rights to be bought, sold, and traded as private property. The system was designed to encourage efficient allocation through market mechanisms.

In practice, it has concentrated water ownership in the hands of large agricultural and mining interests. Smallholder farmers and indigenous communities — particularly in the Maule and Bío Bío regions, where much of Chile's wine production is concentrated — have seen their access to water decline as rights are traded away to larger players.

During the current drought, the contradictions of the system have sharpened: water rights that were allocated decades ago, under assumptions of much higher rainfall, now entitle their holders to volumes of water that no longer physically exist. The legal right to water has decoupled from the hydrological reality.

## Argentina: The Efficiency Law

Argentina's wine industry — concentrated in Mendoza, which produces roughly 70% of the country's wine — depends almost entirely on irrigation from Andes snowmelt. The snowpack has declined by approximately 30% since 1990, and the glaciers that feed Mendoza's rivers are retreating at an accelerating rate.

In response, the Argentine government drafted a Water-Use Efficiency Law in 2025–2026 that would mandate drip irrigation for all new vineyard plantings and impose volumetric water pricing for the first time. The law faces fierce opposition from producers who argue that it imposes costs they cannot absorb, particularly smaller growers already struggling with drought and inflation.

The core tension is between the collective need to conserve a shrinking resource and the individual producer's need to survive the current season. The law attempts to resolve this with a phased approach — but the phases are measured in years, and the drought is measured in months.

## California: Tribal Water Rights

California's water-rights system is one of the most complex in the world, combining riparian rights (tied to land adjacent to water sources), appropriative rights (first-in-time, first-in-right), and federally reserved tribal water rights that date back to the establishment of reservations.

In 2025, several California tribes filed lawsuits asserting their senior water rights in watersheds that supply Napa and Sonoma vineyards. The legal principle is well-established: tribal water rights are federal rights that cannot be lost through non-use, and they are senior to most appropriative rights held by agricultural users.

The lawsuits do not target wine producers specifically — they assert rights across entire watersheds. But wine grapes are the highest-value crop in the affected watersheds, and any reallocation of water will disproportionately affect vineyards. The cases are likely to take years to resolve, but they signal a broader reckoning: California's water allocation system was built for a climate that no longer exists.

## What's Next

The three crises share a common architecture: legal frameworks designed for hydrological stability are colliding with hydrological instability. In each case, the question is not whether water will be reallocated — scarcity makes that inevitable — but how, to whom, and who bears the cost.

For the wine industry, the implications are existential. The regions that produce the world's most famous wines are also the regions where water is becoming most contested. The 632 litres in every bottle is not just a number — it is a claim on a resource that is running out.
