It takes approximately 632 litres of water to produce a single 0.75L bottle of wine. That figure — a global average that includes both vineyard irrigation and winery use — is the axis around which three simultaneous water crises are rotating.

In Chile, Argentina, and California — three of the world's great wine-producing regions — the water is running out. Precipitation is 30–40% below the 30-year average. The Andean snowpack, which feeds Mendoza's irrigation canals and the Maipo Valley's rivers, is in significant decline. And the legal frameworks that govern who gets what water were designed for a different century.

The physical crisis and the legal crisis are converging. The result is not a story about terroir. It is a story about who owns the ground beneath it.

## Chile: The Privatised Basin

Chile's 1981 Water Code — enacted under Pinochet and still largely in force — privatised water rights, allowing them to be bought, sold, and traded independently of land ownership. The result, four decades later, is that mining companies and large agribusiness hold more than 80% of legal water allocations in the Maipo and Aconcagua basins — the primary water sources for Chile's flagship wine regions.

Hydrological models from the UN-FAO project a 30–40% reduction in runoff in the Aconcagua basin by 2026. The basin supplies both the Maipo Valley wine region and the mining sector, setting up a direct competition between two of Chile's largest export industries.

In 2025, Mapuche communities in Araucanía won a landmark court ruling that temporarily halted two water-right sales affecting tributaries of the Bío-Bío River, pending environmental impact assessment. It was the first major Indigenous water-rights intervention in Chile's modern legal history. The ruling does not resolve the crisis — it pauses it, pending evidence — but it establishes a precedent: water rights are not simply property. They carry obligations.

## Argentina: The Draft Law

Argentina's federal government is drafting a Water-Use Efficiency Law that would require water-budget audits for all vineyards over 50 hectares. The law, expected to be enacted in 2026, represents the first systematic attempt to regulate agricultural water use in Mendoza — a province that produces more than 70% of Argentina's wine and depends almost entirely on Andean meltwater for irrigation.

The law's central mechanism is the water budget: every large vineyard must account for its water use against the available supply in its basin. Where consumption exceeds a sustainable allocation, the producer must present a reduction plan. The law does not cap water use — it mandates transparency and planning. In a region where water has historically been treated as free and infinite, that is a significant shift.

## California: The Tribal Claims

California's groundwater regime still operates on a "first-in-first-served" basis — Gold Rush-era rules with no statewide cap on extraction. A 12% increase in new vineyard well permits was recorded in 2025, even as the state entered its latest drought cycle.

In 2025, tribal nations including the Yurok, Hoopa, and Pomo filed lawsuits alleging that commercial vineyards are diverting water that should flow to tribal lands under historic water-rights claims. The cases invoke federal reserved water rights — the principle that when the U.S. government established tribal reservations, it implicitly reserved sufficient water to support them. That principle, established in the 1908 *Winters* Supreme Court decision, is now being applied to the North Coast wine industry.

The lawsuits are in early stages. Their outcomes will take years to resolve. But like the Mapuche ruling in Chile, they signal a shift: water rights are no longer just a contest between agricultural users. They are a contest between competing legal systems, one of which — Indigenous water law — predates the wine industry by centuries.

The three crises are structurally similar: a physical water shortage collides with a legal framework that did not anticipate it. In each case, the law is being stretched, challenged, or rewritten — not by legislators, but by courts. The Mapuche ruling in Chile, the draft law in Argentina, and the tribal lawsuits in California are different legal instruments, but they share a common logic: water is no longer free, no longer infinite, and no longer the exclusive domain of the people who happen to own the land above it.

For the wine industry, the implication is clear. Terroir — the idea that a wine expresses the specific character of the place it comes from — depends on a stable relationship between soil, climate, and water. When that relationship breaks down, terroir becomes a legal question as much as a geological one.

The fight is not about wine. It is about who owns the water underneath it. And in all three regions, the answer is no longer obvious.
