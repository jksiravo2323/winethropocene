## Overview

A band of intensifying aridity is tightening across southern Europe, from Portugal to Cyprus, compressing the southern boundary of European viticulture. This is the warm edge of the wine belt, and it is shrinking. Rising temperatures, declining rainfall, and longer, more intense drought periods are combining to create conditions that exceed the adaptive capacity of traditional varieties and farming systems.

## The Physical Trend

The Mediterranean basin is warming roughly 20% faster than the global average. Precipitation in the region has declined by 5–10% since 1950, with the decline concentrated in the growing season months of April–September. The combination of higher temperatures and lower rainfall creates an aridity feedback loop: higher temperatures increase evapotranspiration, which dries soils, which reduces the cooling effect of soil moisture even when total rainfall holds steady. When rainfall also declines — as it has across much of the southern Mediterranean — the effect is compounded.

## What's at Stake

Sicily is a case study in acceleration. The island has always been hot and dry — that is what makes Nero d'Avola and Frappato possible. But the past decade has brought record-breaking heatwaves, multi-year drought, and an average harvest date that has advanced by nearly three weeks since 1990. Some growers are moving vineyards to higher elevations on Mount Etna. Others are experimenting with heat-tolerant varieties. A few are abandoning viticulture entirely.

The pattern repeats across the Mediterranean arc: Spain's Jerez, Cyprus, Lebanon's Bekaa Valley — all confronting conditions slipping out of range.
