## Overview

The Mediterranean Basin is warming 20% faster than the global average. Across southern Europe — from the Alentejo in Portugal, through Andalusia, Sicily, and the Peloponnese, to Cyprus and the Levantine coast — a band of intensifying aridity is compressing the southern boundary of European viticulture.

The mechanics are simple and relentless: higher temperatures increase evapotranspiration, reducing soil moisture even when total rainfall holds steady. When rainfall also declines — as it has across much of the southern Mediterranean — the effect is compounded. The result is a set of wine regions where the conditions that defined them for centuries are slipping out of range.

## What's at Stake

Sicily is a case study in acceleration. The island has always been hot and dry — that is what makes Nero d'Avola and Frappato possible. But the past decade has brought record-breaking heatwaves, multi-year drought, and an average harvest date that has advanced by nearly three weeks since 1990. Some growers are moving vineyards to higher elevations on Mount Etna. Others are experimenting with heat-tolerant varieties. A few are abandoning viticulture entirely.

The pattern repeats across the Mediterranean arc. Spain's Jerez region is confronting the possibility that the Palomino grape may no longer be viable in its historic home. Cyprus, with its 5,000-year history of wine production, is contending with average temperatures that have risen roughly 1.5°C since the mid-20th century. In Lebanon, the Bekaa Valley's vineyards sit at 900–1,500 metres, and even at those elevations, water stress is becoming chronic.

## The Adaptive Response

The Mediterranean wine industry is not passive. Adaptation strategies include: canopy management to reduce evapotranspiration, deficit irrigation where water is available, variety switching (from Grenache to later-ripening varieties in some zones), and, in the most extreme cases, vineyard relocation to higher elevations. The question is not whether adaptation is possible. It is whether the rate of adaptation can match the rate of change.
