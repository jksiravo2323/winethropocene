## Overview

Santorini makes wine without irrigation. The volcanic island has no rivers and no usable aquifer; its tap water is not potable. The vines survive on rainfall and on the moisture they pull from Aegean night air — captured by the *kouloura*, a low basket-training system that coils each vine into a circle close to the ground, sheltering the fruit from wind and trapping condensation. Some vines are nearly two centuries old.

The grape is Assyrtiko: high-acid, saline, capable of ageing for decades. It defines the island as completely as the island defines it.

## What's at Stake

The rain-fed model is breaking. Annual rainfall has stayed below 200mm for three consecutive years, and the vineyard has contracted from roughly 4,000 hectares in the 1960s to about 1,000 today — drought cutting yields while tourism (around 3.4 million visitors a year) converts farmland to development.

The 2025 vintage made the trajectory legible: an island-wide harvest of about 450 tonnes, roughly 15% of the 2000 level, with Assyrtiko falling from around 2,500 tonnes in 2022 to about 500. Quality stayed high; quantity collapsed. Grape prices reached about €10 per kilo, and fewer than two dozen producers remain.

## Why Santorini Matters

Most regions meet drought by irrigating. Santorini has nothing to pump and a tradition built on dry-farming, which makes it the control case for the whole atlas: viticulture with the irrigation escape hatch removed. Producers have begun using the word "extinction" out loud. Whether the island proves them wrong will depend on plant material, technique, and how much land tourism leaves behind.
