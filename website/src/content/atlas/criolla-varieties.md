## Overview

The Criolla family of grape varieties were brought to South America by Spanish missionaries in the 16th century and have been cultivated continuously for nearly 500 years. For most of that time, they were dismissed as rustic, low-quality grapes — suitable for bulk wine, not fine wine.

That is changing. As climate stress intensifies in the Andes, Criolla varieties are being rediscovered as climate-resilient alternatives to European imports. They are drought-tolerant, adapted to high-altitude growing conditions, and genetically diverse — a living library of traits that European viticulture lost during the phylloxera bottleneck of the late 19th century.

## The Rediscovery

Argentine winemakers are leading the Criolla renaissance, producing single-variety wines from Criolla Chica, Criolla Grande, and Torrontés that showcase the varieties' potential. These are not heritage projects — they are climate-adaptation strategies dressed as wine.
