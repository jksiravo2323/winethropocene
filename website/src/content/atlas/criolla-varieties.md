## Overview

The Criolla family of grape varieties — Criolla Chica, Criolla Grande, Torrontés, Pedro Giménez, Cereza, and others — was brought to South America by Spanish colonisers in the 16th century. For centuries, these varieties formed the backbone of Argentine and Chilean wine production: high-yielding, drought-tolerant, and well-adapted to the semi-arid conditions of the Andean foothills.

For much of the 20th century, Criolla varieties were marginalised. The Argentine wine industry pivoted toward international varieties — Malbec, Cabernet Sauvignon, Chardonnay — that commanded higher prices in export markets. Criolla plantings declined sharply. Many sub-varieties were lost.

## The Rediscovery

That is now reversing. A new generation of Argentine winemakers is rediscovering Criolla varieties — not as nostalgia, but as climate adaptation. Criolla Chica and Criolla Grande are naturally drought-resistant, suited to the semi-arid conditions that are intensifying as the Andes snowpack declines. They require less irrigation than Malbec. They produce wines of distinctive character — lighter-bodied, high-acid, often with herbal and floral notes that are unlike anything produced by international varieties.

The rediscovery is driven by a combination of market differentiation and climate pragmatism. As water becomes scarcer and more expensive in Mendoza, varieties that thrive with less of it become more attractive. As the global market for Malbec matures, producers are looking for distinctive wines that tell a specifically Argentine story.

## What's at Stake

The Criolla varieties represent a form of climate adaptation that is also a form of cultural reclamation: varieties that arrived with colonisation, adapted to a new continent over five centuries, and are now being reclaimed as assets rather than liabilities.

The question is whether the market will follow. Criolla wines are unfamiliar to most consumers. Building demand takes time. But in a world where the climate is shifting faster than vineyards can be replanted, having five centuries of local adaptation in the ground is an asset that few wine regions possess.
