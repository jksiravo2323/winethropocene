## Overview

Champagne is the world's most famous sparkling wine region — and one of the most tightly regulated. The AOC (Appellation d'Origine Contrôlée) defines not only the permitted grape varieties and production methods, but the very geography of the region: a specific set of villages, slopes, and soils in northeastern France where the climate has historically been marginal for still wine production.

That marginality is what made Champagne possible. The region's cool climate produced grapes with high acidity and low sugar — ideal for sparkling wine. But the climate is warming. Harvest dates in Champagne are two to three weeks earlier than in 1980. Sugar levels are rising, acidity falling. The conditions that defined the region are shifting.

## The Voltis Experiment

In 2021, the INAO approved Voltis — a PIWI (fungus-resistant) hybrid grape — for experimental use in Champagne, making it the first new variety permitted in the AOC in decades. Voltis was selected for its resistance to downy and powdery mildew, the two major fungal diseases that pressure Champagne's vineyards in increasingly warm, wet growing seasons.

The approval is experimental: plantings are limited, and Voltis cannot appear on the label. But its presence signals that even Champagne — the most tradition-bound AOC in France — is preparing for a different future.

## The AOC Question

Champagne's AOC is defined partly by climate: the "marginal" cool conditions that produce the region's characteristic acidity. As those conditions shift northward, the legal definition of Champagne remains fixed to its 1927 boundaries. The result is a growing tension between the legal Champagne and the climatic Champagne.

The region is adapting — earlier harvests, canopy management, reserve wine systems — but the boundary stays fixed at its 1927 lines while the climate walks north out of them. At some point the legal Champagne and the climatic Champagne are no longer the same place.
