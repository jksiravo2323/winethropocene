## Overview

PIWI stands for *Pilzwiderstandsfähig* — German for "fungus-resistant." The term describes a family of interspecific hybrid grape varieties bred by crossing traditional *Vitis vinifera* with other *Vitis* species (*labrusca*, *rotundifolia*, and others) to introduce natural resistance to the major fungal diseases that afflict grapevines.

The breeding programmes began in the mid-20th century, concentrated in Germany, Switzerland, and eastern Europe, where cool, wet climates made fungal disease a constant threat. For decades, PIWIs were treated as curiosities — useful for hobbyists, not serious winemakers. That is changing.

## The Fungicide Equation

PIWI varieties can reduce fungicide applications by 60–80% compared to traditional *Vitis vinifera* varieties in high-pressure seasons. In organic and biodynamic viticulture, where synthetic fungicides are not permitted, PIWIs can mean the difference between a viable harvest and a lost one.

As climate change makes growing seasons warmer and wetter across Europe's traditional wine regions, fungal disease pressure is increasing. The varieties that defined Bordeaux, Champagne, and Burgundy evolved in a cooler, drier climate than the one they now inhabit. PIWIs are the adaptation — not a replacement for tradition, but an insurance policy against a changing disease environment.

## The Regulatory Breakthrough

The most significant development for PIWIs is regulatory: in 2021, the INAO approved Voltis for experimental use in Champagne. In 2025–2026, six PIWI varieties were approved for Bordeaux AOC wines, capped at 5% of vineyard area and 10% of any blend. These approvals carry enormous symbolic weight — if PIWIs are permitted in the world's most regulated wine regions, they can be permitted anywhere.

The caps are deliberately restrictive, and the varieties cannot appear on labels. But the door is open, and the disease pressure that opened it is not going to ease.

## Not the First Time

Europe has reached for American grape genetics before. When the phylloxera louse — an insect, not a disease — destroyed much of the continent's vineyard from the 1860s onward, growers in northern Italy planted American direct-producer varieties such as Clinton, Isabella, and Baco by the thousand. They were later marginalised, taxed, and in places banned, remembered as a wartime expedient rather than a tradition. The current PIWI generation is the same idea engineered with precision: not whole American vines, but targeted crossings that carry the resistance gene and little else.

## The Italian Front

The commercial test is already running. In 2024 the UK supermarket Tesco released a wine made from Floréal — a disease-resistant variety on a mainstream shelf, not in a research plot. In Veneto and Friuli, a growing number of producers plant PIWIs without treating them as *uve di serie B*, second-class grapes; much of the breeding and propagation runs through Vivai Cooperativi Rauscedo, the Friulian nursery that supplies resistant vines across Europe.

The next test is harder. Today's PIWIs resist disease but carry generic names and generic flavours. The work now underway at nurseries like Rauscedo is to breed resistance *into* the varieties that define specific appellations — a resistant Glera for Prosecco, a resistant Sangiovese, a resistant Nebbiolo. When those exist and are permitted, the 5% cap stops being the story.
