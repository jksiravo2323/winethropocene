## Overview

Warmer, wetter growing seasons are expanding the range and severity of fungal and bacterial vine diseases — the single largest input cost in viticulture. Powdery mildew (*Erysiphe necator*) and downy mildew (*Plasmopara viticola*) are the primary threats. Both thrive in warm, humid conditions and both are developing resistance to the fungicides used to control them.

Trunk diseases — Esca, Eutypa dieback, Botryosphaeria dieback — are also rising. These chronic infections, spread by rain-splash and pruning wounds, are impossible to treat once established. Warmer winters allow the pathogens to overwinter more successfully; wetter springs provide more infection opportunities.

Vector-borne diseases like Flavescence Dorée — spread by the leafhopper *Scaphoideus titanus* — are expanding their range northward as temperatures rise. Northern Italy and southern France, where the disease was once unknown, are now endemic zones. The vector's range is expected to expand by 200–300 km northward per degree of warming.
