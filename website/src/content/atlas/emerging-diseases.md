## Overview

Climate change is not only expanding the geographic range of viticulture — it is expanding the range of the organisms that attack it. Warmer winters mean fewer pathogen-killing frosts. Wetter springs and summers create ideal conditions for fungal spore germination. Longer growing seasons give diseases more generations per year to evolve and spread.

The result is a triple threat: diseases are appearing in regions where they were previously rare, the severity of outbreaks is increasing, and the chemical tools used to control them are losing effectiveness as pathogens evolve resistance.

## The Major Threats

**Powdery mildew** (*Erysiphe necator*) is the most economically significant fungal disease of grapevines globally. It thrives in warm, humid conditions — exactly the conditions becoming more common across European wine regions. Cases are increasing in Bordeaux, Burgundy, and Champagne, regions where cool, dry summers once kept the pathogen in check.

**Downy mildew** (*Plasmopara viticola*) requires wet leaf surfaces for infection. As rainfall patterns intensify — fewer but heavier rain events — downy mildew pressure is becoming more episodic but more severe. The 2024–2025 season saw significant outbreaks across northern Italy and southern France.

**Grapevine trunk diseases** — Esca, Eutypa dieback, and Botryosphaeria dieback — are chronic, fungal infections that kill vines over years. They are spreading globally, and there is no effective chemical treatment. Warmer temperatures appear to accelerate symptom expression.

**Flavescence dorée**, a phytoplasma disease spread by leafhoppers, is moving north in Europe as the vector's range expands with rising temperatures. It is a quarantine-level disease: infected vineyards must be grubbed up.

## The Adaptation Pipeline

The Bordeaux PIWI approval (2025–2026) is the most significant institutional response to disease pressure. By permitting disease-resistant hybrid varieties — even at a 5% cap — the INAO has opened a door that was closed for nearly a century. Fungicide resistance monitoring programmes across Europe are tracking the emergence of resistant pathogen strains. Breeding programmes in Germany, Switzerland, and Italy are producing new PIWI varieties with resistance to multiple diseases. The race between pathogen evolution and human intervention is accelerating.
