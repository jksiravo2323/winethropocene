## Overview

The Uco Valley, in Argentina's Mendoza province, produces some of the world's most distinctive high-altitude wines. Vineyards sit between 900 and 1,500 metres above sea level, where intense sunlight, wide diurnal temperature swings, and alluvial soils produce Malbecs of remarkable concentration and freshness.

The valley depends almost entirely on meltwater from the Andes for irrigation — and that water is disappearing. The central Andes snowpack has declined by approximately 30% since 1990, according to the Argentine Institute for Snow Research. Glaciers that feed the Mendoza and Tunuyán rivers are retreating. The region entered its fourth consecutive year of drought in 2025.

## The Water Equation

Mendoza receives less than 250mm of rainfall annually — a desert. Without irrigation, viticulture at commercial scale is impossible. The entire system depends on snow accumulating in the high Andes during winter, then melting through spring and summer to feed rivers and irrigation canals.

As temperatures rise, three things happen simultaneously: less snow falls, more of what falls melts earlier, and evaporation increases. The result is less water reaching vineyards when they need it most — during the hot, dry months of December through March, when grapes are ripening.

## Adaptation in Progress

Producers are responding along several fronts:

- **Dry-farming experiments** — Some growers are reducing irrigation to force roots deeper, seeking natural water tables. This produces lower yields but more concentrated fruit.
- **Drought-resistant rootstocks** — Rootstock trials are underway to identify varieties that perform under reduced water availability.
- **Varietal shifts** — There is growing interest in Mediterranean varieties (Garnacha, Monastrell) that are naturally more drought-tolerant than Malbec.
- **Water infrastructure** — Larger producers are investing in reservoir storage and drip-irrigation efficiency. Smaller growers have fewer options.

## What's at Stake

The Uco Valley represents a specific intersection of terroir and climate that may not survive in its current form. The wines it produces — high-altitude Malbecs of power and precision — depend on a set of conditions that are shifting faster than the industry can adapt.

The question is not whether the Uco Valley will continue to produce wine. It will. The question is what kind of wine, at what cost, and for whom.
