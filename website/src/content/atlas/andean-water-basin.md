## Overview

The Andean Water Basin is not a wine region in the conventional sense. It is the hydrological system that makes wine possible in one of the world's great wine regions. The basin spans the high Andes of Argentina and Chile, where winter snow accumulates, glaciers store water over decades, and spring melt feeds the rivers — the Mendoza, Tunuyán, Diamante, Atuel — that irrigate the vineyards of Mendoza province.

Mendoza receives less than 250mm of rainfall annually. Without irrigation from Andean meltwater, commercial viticulture at its current scale is impossible. The entire system — the Malbecs, the high-altitude Cabernets, the Criolla rediscovery — rests on a hydrological foundation that is shrinking.

## The Decline

The central Andes snowpack has declined by approximately 30% since 1990, according to the Argentine Institute for Snow Research (IANIGLA). Glaciers that feed the Mendoza and Tunuyán rivers are retreating. The UN-FAO projects a 30–40% reduction in runoff in the Aconcagua basin — which supplies both the Maipo Valley in Chile and the Mendoza system in Argentina — by 2026.

The mechanism is straightforward: as temperatures rise, less snow falls, more of what falls melts earlier, and evaporation increases. The result is less water reaching vineyards when they need it most — during the hot, dry months of December through March, when grapes are ripening.

## The Response

Producers are adapting along several fronts: dry-farming experiments that force roots deeper, drought-resistant rootstock trials, varietal shifts toward Mediterranean grapes (Garnacha, Monastrell), and investment in reservoir storage and drip-irrigation efficiency.

But adaptation has limits. You cannot replace a glacier with a reservoir. The scale of the Andean water system — and the scale of the wine industry that depends on it — means that individual producer adaptations, however effective, cannot offset the systemic decline of the water source itself.

## What's at Stake

The Andean Water Basin is the largest-scale climate threat to any single wine region in the world. Mendoza produces more than 70% of Argentina's wine and is the world's largest Malbec producer. Its dependence on a single, shrinking water source is not a local problem — it is a structural vulnerability in the global wine supply.

Mendoza can adapt, and is adapting. What no producer can do is out-engineer the arithmetic — the vineyards draw on a water source shrinking faster than reservoirs and rootstocks can offset. What "wine from Mendoza" means in 2050 will be settled in the snowpack, not the cellar.
